# Onboarding

Sadly, most companies fail on this topic. Sometimes the reason is the lack of resources, some others is not giving the importance it has. But reality show us once and again that this is crucial for the future of your teams and your company productivity.

Think about the onboarding as a productivity accelerator, the sooner the new joiner understands the context, the product or service, and the expectation, the sooner this person will become productive and integrated, and this, in the end, is what you expect from the growth of your company. Most companies have a probation period, but it would be unfair to evaluate anyone without the opportunity of understanding where this person is, and what is expected.

A good analogy of starting in a new company could be starting a new relation. At the beginning we need to know each other, understand what the other one expects from you, fall in love somehow. When you start in a new company both should be evaluating the counterpart and feeling the care and the attention. In the case of a company, this is done mainly on the onboarding. Remember that there is only one chance to give a good impression, so take it seriously and prepare the joining of your people. 

On the other part, you, as a new joiner, should expect attention, information, and clarity, but you should also provide with interest, evolution, curiosity and integration.

## As a company

The onboarding plan should be an integral and important part of your standard procedures, it has a preparation for the onboarding, an onboarding phase, and a post onboarding check. Let's talk about it.

### Preparation for the onboarding

This is the easiest part, yet many companies fail on the preparation. This should include:

- Hardware assets: It is unacceptable that a new joiner does not have all the assets ready, that includes computer, mobile phone, and any other hardware tool that the new joiner will need to develop its functions, but also includes access to the premises, designate a person to receive the new joiner, and a working space ready to be occupied.
- Welcome message: Before the starting day, but close to it, the new joiner should receive a welcome message with the specific instructions to follow on the first day of work.
- Permissions: Be sure this person has an active account in the company and all the required permissions to access to the company resources.
- Context Documentation: Prepare an org chart and the initial documents to provide with the first context.
- Select a Mentor: Design a person with experience enough to be the buddy/mentor of the new joiner who will be answering the basic questions and walk the new joiner though the company premises.

### Onboarding Plan

#### Learning and setup

The **first week** is the settle down week, the time to make the first references, and consolidate the idea that it was a good decision to join the company. This will be the time to explain what is the company mission, where this new persons fit into the team structure, talk about the ways of working, and define the communication channels. If there are decisions to make like deciding between the options the company benefits offer, this is the moment to make it.

During the week you should schedule meetings with this person manager or team lead, to review the role expectations, how this person is going to be evaluated, and what is the first expectations in terms of deliverable you have if any. Review the tools the company issues and inform about the workflows, restrictions and security rules the company has.

As soon as possible introduce the new joiner to the assigned buddy or mentor, and encourage them to have a coffee to start in a less formal way and know each other. Buddies with cultural, language, or age similar to the new joiner will help on this step.

The following **three weeks after**, are the right time to move from context to actual production topics. Along with the team have a more informal meeting where everyone can introduce themselves, explain what are they working on, and give a short historical context to understand where they come from, and where they want to go. During this time, no real production should be expected, it is the "understanding time" that happens by shadowing experienced teammates and start joining the regular meetings to get up to speed.

During this time, someone in the team, if the mentor/buddy is in the team would be the ideal person, can introduce a small real task to work on, which should provide close support, review the work done, clarify what was not clear and help on the fixing of the possible mistakes.

By the end of these three weeks, the first month will be completed, and a quick check-in should happen with the manager. This is a critical part of the onboarding, but also to the people management in general, good, recursive, and constructive feedbacks.

#### Guided contributions

By this time you should start having the perception if the new joiner fits in the team or there are some issues. In any case nothing is final, but this is a great moment to list your concerns or clarifications, to be commented in the next 1:1.

During this **second month**, the main goals are:

- Increase ownership with medium-sized tasks or a small project.
- Deepen training on tools, architecture, processes, or domain knowledge.
- Review progress against goals and adjust the plan if needed.
- Ask for feedback on onboarding so far and improve weak spots early.

The main objective of this phase is to get the needed information about the progress and the joiner feelings. Also, regular weekly meetings with the team to get feedback of how is the integration going is crucial, not to make a decision about the continuity but to raise any issue as soon as possible so it can be mitigated and have the smoothest onboarding possible.

At the end of the period, again, the manager should have a complete feedback session with the employee to sumarize the status of the process and ensure the company is fulfilling all the expectations.

#### Independence and ownership

The last part of the onboarding could be considered the take-off for the new joiner. Consolidation of the expectations for the role and level, integration into the team, commitment with the project and the company, and quality and value of the production. To be able to make it happen it is essential to follow these steps:

- Give the person clearer independence and end-to-end responsibility.
- Confirm they understand priorities, quality standards, and team expectations.
- Do a formal 90-day review on progress, gaps, and next growth steps.
- Close onboarding with a plan for the next quarter, not just a “welcome done” moment.

By the end of this last onboarding part, the new joiner is not going to be a new joiner anymore, but a team member in all aspects. That is why it is so important to do a correct closing onboarding to avoid any leftovers on confusion, unclarity, or misunderstanding, and put them "to work".

### Benefits of a good onboarding

When an employee feels valued, attended, and listened, this **consolidates the feeling of belonging**. I've seen many times people thrown to the teams with a bunch of documentation to read, that spend most of the time trying to understand what is expected to do, asking the wrong people for help, and by the wrong people I don't mean bad people, just people that are not the most qualified to answer those questions, or people who never had a proper onboarding too, and that leads to a gap between what you, as the hiring part, and the joiner, the hired part, on what is really expected and how to do what is asked to do. When done in the right way, this **will affect retention and turnover**.

As commented before, you only have one chance to give a good first impression, and this is crucial, to perceive that you joined a structured and people-oriented organization will start shaping a **long-term engagement**.

We could say that there are two main benefits of doing a proper onboarding, the first one is that it will reduce the time to productivity, as clear and good information will speed time to production. The second one is that gives you the right information about the fit of the new team members. A professional that cannot progress the onboarding plan, will rarely make it later, and this plan also can be designed to check the real cultural fit. But bare in mind that cultural fit does not meam "is like we all are here", we'll dive in this concept later.

The cost of bad onboarding for IT teams is high. Frustration, impostor syndrome, feeling of not belonging, and many other problems. So keep in mind that it is better to do none, than to do a bad one.

### Onboarding plan example

This can be a starting point for an onboarding plan. What this means is that this plan is expected to be customized to your company needs, not to be used as is.

**30 days**
**Goal**: Learn the company, team, tools, and basic workflows.

- Understand the company mission, product, and IT team structure.
- Set up all accounts, permissions, devices, and required tools.
- Learn internal processes, ticketing, escalation paths, and security rules.
- Meet key stakeholders and teammates.
- Complete initial training and required compliance tasks.
- Shadow a teammate and observe common work patterns.
- Deliver one small, low-risk task with support.

**60 days**
**Goal**: Start taking ownership of real work.

- Handle common tasks with less supervision.
- Learn deeper system knowledge, architecture, or support processes.
- Complete medium-complexity assignments.
- Join regular meetings and contribute ideas.
- Identify recurring issues and possible improvements.
- Ask for feedback from manager and teammates.
- Show steady progress on speed, quality, and confidence.

**90 days**
**Goal**: Work independently and deliver consistent value.

- Own end-to-end tasks or a small project.
- Resolve most routine issues without help.
- Understand team priorities, standards, and escalation rules.
- Contribute to process improvements or documentation.
- Demonstrate good communication and reliability.
- Review strengths, gaps, and next development goals with the manager.
- Agree on the next quarter’s priorities.

To complete this planning, consider adaptations based on:

- Role-specific skills.
- Systems and tools to learn.
- Stakeholders to meet.
- Training courses to complete.
- Metrics to track.
- Manager check-in dates.
